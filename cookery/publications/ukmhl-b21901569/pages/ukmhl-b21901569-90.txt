Passing rapidly on without naming the ordinary and well-known service of cold meats, fresh and preserved, poultry and game, open or under paste, in some form, to be found in profusion on table or sideboard, and in which this country is unrivalled, a hint or two relating to some lighter cold entrees may be suggested. It is scarcely possible to treat these apart from the salad which, admirable by itself, also forms the natural garnish for cold dishes. A simple aspic jelly, Httle more than the consomme of yesterday flavoured with a little lemon-peel and tarragon vinegar, furnishes another form of garnish, or a basis for pre- senting choice morsels in tempting forms, such as poultry livers, ox-palates, quenelles, fillets of game, chicken, wild fowl, fish, prawns, plover's eggs, &c., associated with a well-made salad. On this system an enterprising cook can furnish many changes of light but excellent nutritious dishes. On salad so much has been written, that one might suppose, as of many other cuHnary productions, that to make a good one was the result of some difficult and complicated process, instead of being simple and easy to a degree.
