In order to arrange a dinner some practical acquaintance with food is necessary—Also of the season at which various kinds are in perfection—The art of menu writing—The scheme or elementary outline of a dinner—Mejius should be written in French—Examples of simple menus for each month in the year—Some in English, chiefly in French, for comparison—Menus of higher character—Further remarks on the order in which dishes should be presented—And on other matters arising out of the subject. The remark is frequently heard from the domestic head of an English family, the lady of Alleged the house—who, although in easy composfng a circumstances and much occupied in menu, good society, does not care to delegate her authority to a skilled professional housekeeper—that nothing puzzles her more than the composition of menus for the inevitable dinner parties. She feels almost as ready, or rather as unready, as she would be if called upon to compose a sonnet or a symphony. Her husband, whose counsels are so valuable, or at all events are so promptly furnished on almost all other topics, utterly fails in his attempt to offer a suggestion now.
