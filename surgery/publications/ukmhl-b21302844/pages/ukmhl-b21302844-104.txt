Peacock (‘ St. Thomas’s Hosp. Bep.,’ iii,i) deduces the following conclusions from 87 cases of acute rheumatism. The disease is more common in men than in women, on account of then greater liability to exposure. The chronic form is most common in old persons, the acute in early life. One attack of the acute affection predisposes to another (in one case there were nine attacks). In much the larger proportion of cases the disease was of a mild or subacute character, the temperature in comparatively few cases exceeding 103° F. In 33.3 per cent, of the whole number of cases, or in one case in three, there was either recent or old cardiac complication. These complications are more frequent in the male than in the female, and in early than m advanced age, but they do not appear to differ much in proportion to the intensity of the rheumatic fever: endocarditis, however, was especially common m the cases of more severe disease, while pericarditis more particularly oc- curred in the slighter cases.
