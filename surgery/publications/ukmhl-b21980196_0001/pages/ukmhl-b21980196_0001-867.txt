Stanley, Edward, Abstract of Lectures delivered before the College of Surgeons; in London Medical Gazette, vol. xx. 1837. A—OF CARIES. (Caries, Lat; Knochenfrass, IJcinfaule, Germ.; Cane, Fr.) 861. Caries consist in a diseased change of the substance of bone, ana- lagous to ulcers in soft parts (1). If the diseased bone be bared of the soft | parts covering it, it is found to be brownish, often blackish, its surface rough and uneven, yielding, worm-eaten and destroyed to a considerable extent; the bony layers are fragile, may be easily penetrated with a silver probe, and a grayish brownish or blackish ichor escapes, which gives out a filthy and peculiar smell; the bony layers are frequently loosened, and 1 spongy or tieshy growths (Caries jungosa) spring up from the surface of the ulcer (2).
