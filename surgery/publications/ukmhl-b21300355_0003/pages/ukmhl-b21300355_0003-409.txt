As soon as the limb has been separated, the surgeon secures the vessels on the posterior flap, partly by his fingers, partly 'by compression with a large sponge, and ligatures are applied as quickly as possible. The femoral is secured last, for, as long as the assistant retains his hold, haemorrhage from it is not to be dreaded. Thus, when both surgeon and assistants are quick and cool, the operation may be completed with the loss of much less blood than might be expected. In cases of accidental injury re- quiring this operation, the lever use of the limb must frequently be wanting ; and in such cases too, the parts may be so injured as not to afford flaps ante- riorly and posteriorly. In these circumstances, the surgeon must be guided by experience and judgment in adopting the mode of procedure which appears most applicable; in ordinary cases the operation above detailed appears the preferable. Excision of diseased portions of hones, is practi- sed occasionally with the view of removing a source of irritation and exhaustion from the system, without sacrifice of a limb. When the operation proves suc- cessful, the beneficial effect on the general health is as remarkable and rapid as after removal of the hectic cause by amputation ; the pulse falls and grows firmer, diarrhoea and sweating cease, the hectic flush leaves the cheek, in short, the constitution makes a complete and successful rally.
