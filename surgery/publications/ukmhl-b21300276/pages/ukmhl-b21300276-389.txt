By the operation of the taxis is meant, the act of returning the protruded viscera into the cavity of the abdomen. To effect which, two things must be attended to, viz.—the position, and direction of force. With respect to the posityiu of the patient, as our aim is to increase the capacity of the abdominal cavity, by relaxing its walls, it is obvious that the head, pelvis, and lower extremities, should be made to approach each other; and as the apertures, through which the hernia has passed, are relaxed, by turning the knee of the extremity of the affected side inwards, this must be done ^ere we attempt reduction. In inguinal hernia of small size, the force must be directed towards the ilium; but not so much so in large hemiae. In femoral, or crural hernia, the tumour turns upon itself, after it has descended a little way in the thigh; thus, the pressure must first be downwards, and when the tumour is brought to the level of Pouparf s ligament, it is to be pushed directly upwards.
