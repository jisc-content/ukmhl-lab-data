The disease supervenes at various periods ; sometimes, though rarely, immedi- ately on the infliction, in other instances after the lapse of eight or ten days, and often when the wound' is healing, or nearly cicatrized. In warm climates, where it is of very frequent occurrence, it occasionally seems to be caused by exposure to damp and change- able weather; in children, it supervenes on the slight- est irritation or excoriation. It is often induced by the presence of splinters, or other bodies of an irritating nature, and by the partial division of. nerves. Not unfrequently it occurs after clean wounds, as ampu- tation or venesection ; in the former it may arise from a nerve being included in the same ligature with an artery, in the latter, from a nervous twig being par- tially divided.
