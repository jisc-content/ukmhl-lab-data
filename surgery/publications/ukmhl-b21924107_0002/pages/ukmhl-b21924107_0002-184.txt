Whilst living in Broad Street, in the city, a lady came to me with a tumour in the side of the neck ; I perceived on the opposite side several scars; I said, Will you allow me to try if I can prevent a scar?‚Äù She answered, it was for that purpose she had consulted me. Warm poultices had been used on the pre- vious occasions: I made a very small open- ing, such as is made in bleeding, and squeezed out the contents, and she got well with scarcely a perceptible scar. It is of the highest importance, then, to en- deavour to prevent those appearances, which, on the exposed parts of the body, produce such painful feelings. In the higher orders of life particularly, a child with scars on its neck would be almost excluded from society. There is a point of great importance to be attended to, that is, the direction in which you make the opening; always make it trans- versely, and not in the axis of the neck; for, when the wound heals, it will be scarcely seen among the creases or folds of the skin.
