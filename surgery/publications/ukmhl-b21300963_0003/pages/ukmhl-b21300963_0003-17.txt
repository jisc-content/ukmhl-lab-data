INTRODUCTORY LECTURES IN THE YEAR 1814. LECTURE I. In succeeding Sir William Rlizard in the honorable office of Professor of Anatomy and Surgery, I think it right to inform my audience that he was my earliest in- structor in these sciences ; and that I am greatly indebted to him for much and most valuable information respecting them. My warmest thanks are also due to him for the interest he excited in my mind to- wards these studies, and for the excellent advice he gave me, in common with other students, to direct me in the attainment ol knowledge. “ Let y°ur search after truth,” he would say, “ be eager and constant.
