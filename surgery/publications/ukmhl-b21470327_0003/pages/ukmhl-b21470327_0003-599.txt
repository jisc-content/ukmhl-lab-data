The former was merely a sort of calefacient plaster, and was applied to parts not covered with liairs. Oribasius says of it that it is prepared like the malagma. When it is desired to make it calefacient, he directs us to add to it pellitory, the seed of rosemary, and bitumen; when desiccative, the sulphur vivum, salts, and the ashes of vegetables ; and when it is wished to make it of such a nature as to scarify the parts, he directs us to add to it limnesium (adarce) and spurge. The part to which it is to be applied, he says, if weak, should be shaven, but in the more obust, and when a stronger application is required, it need not be shaven, unless it is to be applied to the head, chin^ or pubes.
