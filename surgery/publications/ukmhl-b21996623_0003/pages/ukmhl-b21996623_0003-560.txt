Inflammation appears to produce morbid growths; first, by weakening the parts; and secondly, by inducing a temporary modification of the normal mode of nutrition, both which circumstances are the invari- able consequents of inflammation, and may reasonably be presumed to predispose the affected parts to assume a new disposition, corresponding to any preexisting cause of disease which may be in the constitution at the time. Without the concurrence of some other cause, however, inflammation alone is insufficient for the effect, and when this cause actually exists inflammation is unnecessary, and the remedies for inflammation unavailing.
