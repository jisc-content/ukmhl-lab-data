Excoriations, which may develop into true bed-sores, sometimes form where two skin surfaces remain constantly apposed, as in the region of the scrotum or labium, under pendulous mammae, or between fingers or toes confined in a splint. Clinical Features.—Two clinical varieties are met with—the acute and the chronic bed-sore. The acute bed-sore (Fig. 26) usually occurs over the sacrum or buttock. It comes on very rapidly after spinal injuries and in the course of certain brain diseases. Blisters form, and the skin loses its vitality and dies. In advanced cases of general paralysis of the insane, a peculiar form of acute bed-sore beginning as bullae, and passing on to the formation of black, dry eschars, which slowly separate, frequently occurs on such parts as the inner side of the knee, the angle of the scapula, and the heel. The chronic bed-sore (Fig. 27) begins as a dusky reddish purple patch, which gradually becomes darker till it is almost black. The parts around are oedematous, and vesicles may form.
