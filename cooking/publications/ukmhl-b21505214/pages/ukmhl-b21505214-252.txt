Mark, tire greater number of times boiling vinegar is poured over either sort, the sooner it will be ready. To pickle Walnuts. When they will bear a pin to go into them, put a brine of salt and water, boiled, and strong enough to bear an egg on it, being quite cold first. It must be well skimmed while boiling. Let them soak six days; then change the brine, let them stand six more ; then drain them, and pour over them in a jar a pickle of the best white-wine vinegar, with a good quantity of pepper, pimento, ginger, mace, cloves, mustard-seed, and horse- radish ; all boiled together, but cold. To every hun- dred of walnuts put six spoonsful of mustard-seed and two or three heads of garlic or shalot, but the latter is least strong. Thus done, they will be good for several years, if close covered. The air will soften them. They will not be fit to eat under six months. When the walnuts are used, boil up the pickle with half a pound of anchovies to a gallon, and a tea-spoonful of cayenne, and you will have a very good ketchup. Another way.
