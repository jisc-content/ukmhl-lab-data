382 GRAVIES AND SAUCES. Obs.—This sauce is in much esteem in France, where the people of taste, weary of so many rich dishes, to obtain the charm of variety, occasionally order the fare of the peasant. “ The ricli, tir’d with continual feasts, “ For change become their next poor tenant’s guests; “ Drink hearty draughts of Ale from plain brown bowls, “ And snatch the homely rasher from the coals.” Dryden’s Prologue to “ All for Love." (larlick Gravy. — (No. 311.) See also (No. C72.) Slice a pound and a half of veal or beef, and pepper and salt it, lay it in a stewpan with a couple of carrots split, and three turnips, four cloves of garlick sliced, and a large spoonful of water; set the stewpan over a gentle lire in a stove, and watch when the meat begins to slick to the pan; when it does, turn it, and let it be very well browned, (but take care it is not at all burnt;) then pour in a pint and a half of gravy, and put in a bunch of sweet herbs, four blades of mace, a couple of cloves bruised, and slice in a lemon; set it on as:ain, and let it simmer very gently for half an hour longer; throw in a little flour from time to time, till the gravy is
