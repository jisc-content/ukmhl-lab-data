The latter is commonly called lamb fry ; and a dinner may be bought fora few pence. Be sure and ask for the sweet-bread ; for butchers are extremely apt to reserve it for their own use; and therefore lamb fry is almost always sold without it. Fry five or six slices of salt pork; after it is taken out, put in your lamb fry while the fat is hot. Do it thoroughly; but be careful the fire is not too furious, as it is apt to scorch. Take a large handful of parsley, see that it is washed clean, cut if up pretty fine; then pour a little boiling water into the fat in which your dinner has been fried, and let the parsley cook in it a minute or two; then take it out in a spoon, and lay it over your slices of meat. Some people, who like thick gravies, shake in a little flour into the fryingpan, before pouring in the boiling water. Bones from which roasting pieces have been cut, may be bought in the market for a trifle, from which a very rich soup may be made, be- sides skimming off fat for shortening. If the bones left from the rump be bought, they will be found full of marrow, and will give more than 'a pint of good shortening, without injuring the richness of the soup.
