CHAPTER IV. Milk, an example of a natural “ complete” food—Its employment as food, almost universal—Essentially an animal food—Ex- ceedingly susceptible of injury—Mode of distribution, a fertile source of disease—Milk epidemics of fever, &c.—Composition of milk—Value as a diet—Wliey—Koumiss—Means to be adopted in order to secure the purity and wholesomeness of milk. The term “complete,” as we have seen in the fore- going chapter, is employed to denote that the food so designated contains all the elements necessary to the support of the body, and to the activity of its functions. Most commonly, such nourishment is a compound of two or more animal or vegetable substances, combined in order to afford the various elements necessary to meet all the demands of animal life. But there is a notable example of a single animal product, perhaps the best which can be offered as a complete food ; one prepared by nature, furnished in great abundance, and which we are all well acquainted with, namely, milk. It is a product which slightly varies in different species of the mammalian family. That form which we are most familiar with is the milk of the cow, and it may be taken as the type.
