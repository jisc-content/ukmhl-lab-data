If you serve a turbot sauce Hollandaise you must not serve after it a poulet sauce supreme, or even a blanquette de veau. The use of French words in a menu is not indispensable. The delicate eater is not bound to know French. Hors d'ceuvres are either cold, or, in pro- fessional language, hors d'oeuvres d'office, or warm, that is to say, hors d'oeuvres de cui- sine. Formerly warm hors d'oeuvres — al- ways without sauces—were served side by side with the entrees, only on smaller dishes. Nowadays many warm hors d'ceuvres are reckoned as entrees or light entremets, and passed round rapidly, so that they may lose none of their delicacy by standing on the table. At dinner cold hors d'ceuvres offer but lit- tle interest to the gourmet, with the excep- tion of the canteloupe melon and the water- melon, but especially the canteloupe, when just ripe, and with the aroma fully developed.
