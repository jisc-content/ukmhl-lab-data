Grimod de la Reyniere was the sworn enemy of servants, and the dream of his life was to discover some machine to replace those human machines which have always too many eyes and too many ears, and render all expansiveness impossible or imprudent. In 1728 the Margravine of Bayreuth speaks in her curious memoirs of a table de con fiance which was worked by means of pulleys. “No servants are needed,” she says; “ they are replaced by drums placed at the side of the guests, who write what they want on a tablet; the drums de- scend into the kitchen, and ascend again with the objects required.” Forty years later, Loriot, an ingenious man who had discovered
