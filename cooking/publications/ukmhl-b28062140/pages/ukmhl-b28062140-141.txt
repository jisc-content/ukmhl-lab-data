For tea-making and for all delicate cooking operations the water should be caught at the first boil. Nickel or silver pots are unobjec- tionable, provided they be kept scrupulously clean ; but ordinary earthen or porcelain pots are preferable on all accounts. Tea made as above described will be drank with or without loaf-sugar sweetening, and needs no softening and spoiling with milk or cream. The custom of adding cream or milk to tea and coffee doubtless originated in ignorance or bad brewing. The coffee-drinking nations and the tea-drinkers of the East do not know this custom. The Russians put in their tea a slice of lemon. If the tea or coffee be good, the addition of milk spoils the taste. Furthermore, the tannic acid which is contained both in tea and in coffee changes the nature of the albu- minous part of milk, and, so to speak, tans the globules of the milk, and renders them in- digestible.
