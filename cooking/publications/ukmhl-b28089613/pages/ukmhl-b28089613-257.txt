Medical men in the tw elfth and thirteenth centuries, more especially in France, ordered some fruit3 at the commencement of dinner. Champier recommended that cherries, raspberries, peaches, and apricots should be so eaten; and that after dinner medlars, pistachio nuts, filberts, chestnuts, apples, quinces, and pears should be produced. Melon, in the time of Henry IV., and to this day, is eaten in France with the boulli, just after the soup. Sully tells that one day when Henry IV. was at dinner, his Maitre d’Hotel entered with a golden basin filled with melons. “ Right glad am I,” said the jovial king, “ for I wish to-day to have a surfeit.
