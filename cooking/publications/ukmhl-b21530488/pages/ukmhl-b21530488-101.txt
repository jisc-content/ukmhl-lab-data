MEAT. 29 Wash all meat before you dress it; if for boiling, the colour will be better for soaking ; but if for roast- ing, dry it. Boiling in a well-floured cloth will make meat white. Particular care must be taken that the pot is well skimmed the vwment it boils, otherwise the foulness will be dispersed over the meat. The more soups or broth are skimmed, the better and cleaner they will be. The boiler and utensils should be kept delicately clean. Put the meat into cold water, and flour it well first. Meat boiled quick will be hard; but care must be taken that in boiling slow it does not stop, or the meat will be underdone. If the steam is kept in, the water will not lessen m uch ; therefore when you wish it to boil away, toke OS’ the cover of the soup-pot. Vegetables should uot be dressed with the meat, ex- cept carrots or parsnips with boiled beef. As to the length of time required for roasting and boiling, the size of the joint must direct; as also the strength of the fire, the nearness of the meat to it, and in boiling, the regular though slow progress it makes ; for if the cook, when told to hinder the copper from boiling quick, lets it stop from boiling up at all, the usual time will not be sufficient, and the meat will b« underdone.
