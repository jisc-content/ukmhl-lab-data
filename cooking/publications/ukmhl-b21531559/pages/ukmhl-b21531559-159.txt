NECK OF MUTTON is particularly useful, as so many dishes may be made of it; but it is not advantageous for a family. The bones should be cut short, which the butchers will not do, unless particularly desired. I'he best end of the neck may be boiled, and served with turnips j or roasted, or dressed in steaks, in pies, or harrico. The scrags may be stewed in broth j or with a small quantity of water, some small onions, a few pepper- corns, and a little rice, and served together. When a neck is to be boiled to look particularly nice, saw down the chine-bone, strip the ribs half way down, and chop otF the ends of the bones about four inches. The skin should not be taken off till boiled, and then the fat will look the whiter. When there is more fat to a neck or loin of mutton, than it is agreeable to eat with the loin, it makes an uncommonly good suet-pudding, or crust for a meat- pie, if cut very fine.
