The bread should be in separated crumbs, and look light. OMELET. Make a batter of eggs and milk and a very little flour} put to it chopped parsley, green onions, or chives (the latter is best) or a very small quantity of shalot, a little pepper, salt, and a scrape or two of nutmeg. Make some butter boil in a small frying- pan, and pour the above batter into it; when one side is of a fine yellow brown, turn it and do the other. Double it when served. Some scraped lean ham, or grated tongue, put in at first, is a very pleasant addi- tion. Four eggs will make a pretty sized omelet; but many cooks will use eight or ten. A small proportion of flour should be used. If the taste be approved, a little tarragon gives a fine flavour. A good deal of parsley should be used. Ramakins and omelet, though usually served in the course, would be much better if they were sent up after, that they might be eaten as hot as possible. BUTTER TO SERVE AS A LITTLE DISH.
