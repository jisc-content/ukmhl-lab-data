This may be done by filling the digester only three parts full of water and bruised bones or meat, which it is to be noticed are all put in together. It must then be placed near a slow fire, so as only to simmer, for the space of eight or ten hours, as more heat injures the quality. After this has been done, the soup is to be strained through a hair sieve or colander, in order to separate any bits of bones. The soup is then to be again put into the digester; and, after whatever vegetables and seasoning are thought necessary are added, the whole is to be well boiled together for an hour or two, and it will be then fit for immediate use. The bain-marie is a very shallow cistern placed on a hot hearth, and generally made of copper, for containing hot water to keep soups and sauces warm, but may occa- sionally be used for keeping dishes warm. It is the best mode of keeping anything hot which has been suffi- .ciently dressed, but not ready to be sent to table; se the dish, if covered and placed in it, may be thus kept perfactly warm, without being scorched as it would be if put before the fire.
