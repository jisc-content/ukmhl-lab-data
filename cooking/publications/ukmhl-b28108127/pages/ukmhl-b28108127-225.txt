The men of fashion, leisure, or wealth who would formerly have lived at the restaurants now dine at the table d'hote of their clubs, between two feverish seances at the baccarat-table, and thus the restaurants have lost that nucleus of regular and fasti- dious customers which, by its readiness to criticise and appreciate, obliged and en- couraged the chef to keep up the traditions of the dainty palates of the past. At present the great restaurants of Paris depend for support as much on foreigners as on resident Parisians; their patrons are, therefore, un- stable, and the criticism of their cookery less constant and less rigorous than it used to be. Once more the word “ criticism ” flows from the point of my pen, and sums up the whole gist of the preceding pages. Without criti- cism there can be no delicate dining. How often you hear people say, “ Oh ! I am not particular.
