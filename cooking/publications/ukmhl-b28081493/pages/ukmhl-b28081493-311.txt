PEASE PUDDING.—Tie a pint of split peas in a cloth, leaving them room to swell, but not more; put them into a stewpan of cold water, where let them boil nearly half an hour until tender, but not at all watery (which they would not be if allowed only sufficient room to swell, and no more); then turn thein out of the cloth, rub them through a hair sieve into a basin, after which add a quarter of a pound of butter, season with a little white pepper and salt, and mix all well together, with three yolks and one whole egg; lightly flour a pudding- cloth, which lay in a small round-bottomed basin, pour in the mixture, tie up the cloth, and put the pudding to boil for an hour in a saucepan of boiling water; when done, turn it from the cloth upon a dish, and serve with a joint of boiled pork. • '5A5: f0ViL.PILLAU.—Put one pound of the best Patna ce. int0 a fryingpan with two ounces of butter, which keen moving over a slow fire, until the rice is lightly browned- then have ready a fowl trussed as for boiling, whichputTo a^ew- pan, with five pints of good broth; pound in a mortar about Sp P car<?amum.seeds Wlth tlie tusks, half an ounce of coriander seed,, and sufficient cloves, allspice, mace, cinnamon, and pep- percorns, to make two ounces in the aggregate, which tie ud tightly in a cloth, and put into the stewpan with the fowl- let t bofl slowly until the fowl is nearly done, then add the rice which let stew until quite tender and almost dry; have ready foui onions, which cut into slices the thickness of lialf-crowJ pieces, sprinkle over with flour, and fry, without breaking them of a nice brown colour, have also six thin slices of bacon° curled ;ourSm 6 ’ fn1 tW° eggS boiled lmrd; then lay the fowl upon your dish, which cover over with the rice, forming a pyramid garnish with the bacon, fried onions, and the hard-boiled e^s cut into quarters, and serve very hot. co * All rice should he well washed before usirw T
