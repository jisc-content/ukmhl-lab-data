CULINARY JOTTINGS FOR MADRAS. 250 roomy sauce-pan, with a quarter of a pint of water, a pinch of salt, and a dust of black pepper; boil it, and add two ounces of flour. Stir over the Are for four minutes, aud then mix with it two ounces of grated Parmesan, and two eggs, one after the other. Put the paste thus formed on a buttered-baking sheet in lumps the size of a hen’s egg, flatten them slightly, brush them over with an egg, hake in the oven, and serve on a napkin very hot. “ Ramequins en caisses'’:—Take two ounces of mild grated cheese, and two ounces of white bread-crumbs; soak the crumbs in milk, and pound them in a mortar with the cheese, and a little butter, till the whole is well mixed; now season the mixture with pepper and salt, add- ing a tea-spoonful of mustard powder, and the yolks of three eggs. Finally beat up the egg whites to a stiff froth, mingle it with the mixture, and fill your paper cases, which should be well buttered to prevent their burning outside, or “ catching” the fondue within: hake them from ten minutes to a quarter of an hour, and serve them as soon as they have raised their heads, and have slightly taken colour. Beignets d la PignatelU:—Put one pint of water in a stew-pan with one and a half ounces of butter, season with salt and pepper; boil, and add four aud a half ounces of flour, and one ounce of grated Parmesan. Stir over the fire for three minutes, then add sufficient eggs to turn the mixture to a smooth paste. Add to the paste one ounce of lean cooked ham finely chopped. When mixed, form the paste into fritters, and fry in plenty of hot fat. “ Beignets souffles au Parmesan” will be found elsewhere, and several dishes demanding cheese amongst the menus. JJ.B.—The best flour for fondues and souffles is potato flour, a recipe for which will be found in the menus. China souffle cases are nicer than paper ones.
