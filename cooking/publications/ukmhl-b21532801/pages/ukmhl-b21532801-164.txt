Hen-turkey is known by the same rules; but if old, her legs will be red and rough. Fowls.—If a cock is young, liis spurs will be short; but take care to see they have not been cut or pared, which is a trick often practised. If fresh, the vent will be close and dark. Pullets are best just before they be<nn to lay, and yet are full of egg : if old hens, their combs and legs will be rough; if young they will be smooth. A good capon has a thick belly and a large rump: there is a particular fat at his breast, and the comb is very pale. Black-legged fowls are most moist, if for roasting. Geese.—The bill and feet of a young one will be yel- low, and there will be but few hairs upon them; if old, they will be red: if fresh, the feet will he pliable; if stale, dry and stiff. Geese are called green till three or four months old. Green geese should be scalded . a stubble goose should be picked dry'. , Ducks.—Choose them by the same rules, of having supple feet, and by their being hard and thick on the breast and belly. The feet of a tame duck are thick, and inclining to dusky yellow; a wild one has the feet reddish, and smaller than the tame. They should be picked dry. Ducklings must be scalded. Piqeovs should be very fresh; when they look flabby about the vent, and this part is discoloured, they are stale. The feet should be supple; if old the feet are harsh. The tame ones are larger than the wild, and are thought best by some persons; they should be fat and tender; but many are deceived in their size, because a full crop is as large as the whole body of a small pigeon.
