Another, without butter.—Dry one pound of flour, and one and a quarter of sugar; heat seven eggs, yolks and whites apart; grate a lemon, and, with a spoonful of brandy, heat the whole together with your hand for an hour. Bake in a buttered pan, in a quick oven. Sweetmeats may be added, if approved. Tea Cukes. Rub fine four ounces of butter into eight ounces of flour ; mix eight ounces of currants, and six of fine Lis- bon sugar, two yolks and one white of eggs, and a spoonful of brandy. Roll the paste the thickness of an Oliver biscuit, and cut with a wine glass. You may beat the other white, and wash over them; and either dust sugar, or not, as you like. Benton Tea Cakes. Mix a paste of flour, a little bit of butter, and milk : roll as thin as possible, and bake on a back-stone over the fire, or on a hot hearth. Another sort, as Biscuits.—Rub into a pound of flour six ounces of butter, and three large spoonfuls of yeast, and make into a paste, with a sufficient quantity of new milk; make into biscuits, and prick them with a clean fork. Another Sort.—Melt six or 'seven ounces of butter with a sufficiency of new milk wanned to make seven pounds of flour into a stiff paste ; roll thin, and make into biscuits. A Biscuit Cake. One pound of flour, five eggs well beaten and strained, eight ounces of sugar, a little rose or orange-flower wa- ter ; beat the whole thoroughly, and bake one hour.
