FRUITS. “Fruit of all kinds, in coat Rough, or smooth rind, or bearded husk, or shell. She gathers tribute large, and on the board Heaps with unsparing hand.’* —Paradise Lost. Bring me berries or such cooling fruit As the kind, hospitable woods provide. —Cowper. Fruit for preserving should be carefully selected, removing all that are imperfect. They are in the best condition when not fully ripe, and as soon as possible after they are picked. Small fruit should not be allowed to stand over night after they are picked when they are to be preserved. Use only the finest sugar for perserving. When fruit is sealed in glass cans, wrap paper of two or three thicknesses around the cans. The chemical action of light will affect the quality of the preserves when perfectly air-tight; With this precau- tion, glass cans are preferable to any other for preserv- ing fruit. One-half a pound of sugar to a pound of fruit is a good rule for canned fruit, although many house- keepers use but one-quarter of a pound of sugar to a pound of fruit.
