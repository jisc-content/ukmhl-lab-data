SAUCES, GRAVIES, &C. Clove, one ounce. Fennel seed, half an ounce. Anniseed, half an ounce. Beaten into powder, used in the same way as the curry powder. SAVOURY RAGOUT POWDER. (No. 4^7.) Mustard, Allspice, and Black pepper ground and sifted fine, half an ounce each. Ginger, Nutmeg grated, and Salt, a quarter of an ounce each. Mace, two drachms. Cayenne pepper, one drachm. Pound them in a mortar, and pass them through a fine hair sieve : bottle them for use. The above articles will pound much easier and finer if they are dried firsi in a Dutch oven before a very gentle fire, at a good distance from it: if you give them much heat, all the finest flavour of them will be evaporated, and they will soon get a strong rank taste. Obs.—The spices in a ragout are indispens-' able (ogive it a flavour, but not a predona inant one ; their presence should be rather supposed than perceived; they are the invisible spirit of good cookery, and that happy mixture of them, and proportion to each other of the other ingredients which produces an exquisitely delicious, yet inde- finable flavour, is the “ chef-d'oeuvre” of a first rate cook.
