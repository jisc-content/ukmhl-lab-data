VEAL. (No. 33.) \ eal requires particular care to roast it a fine brown. Let the fire be the same as for beef, in proportion ; a sound large fire for a large joint, and a brisker for a smaller: put it at some distance from the fire to soak thoroughly, and then draw it near to finish it brown. When it is first laid down, it is to be basted with butter; when it is almost done, it is to be basted again, and lightly * dredged with a little flour to froth it nicely. With those joints which are not stuffed, send up cakes or halls of forcemeat, { No. 375,) as garnish to the dish, or fried pork sausages. Bacon and greens are also always expected to attend veal.
