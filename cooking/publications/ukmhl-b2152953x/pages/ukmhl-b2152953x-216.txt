This was called tyrotarichm, and Cicero often employs this word to designate a frugal style of cookery.63 Besides those countries celebrated for the goodness of cheeses already mentioned, there is Tromelia, in Acliaia,54 and the Island of Cythnus, where they represented their cheeses on their money, an ingenious manner of making them known, which succeeded wondeifully well; and lastly, Salonia, a city of Bithynia, renowned for its rich pastures, where numerous herds of cows were kept, and whose milk furnished an exquisite kind known by the name of Salonite cheese.5- It appears to have been often served to the Emperor Augustus, who ate it with brown bread, little fish, and fresh figs. The art of giving a relish to the cheese, by mixing with it odoriferous herbs, is said to be more than nine hundred years old. This operation was designated per siller, showing that originally parsley was introduced.57 We cannot say whether the Romans made use of this plant to gi\e a pungency to their cheese, but it is certain that they often mixed some herbs with it.58 Thus Columella informs us that sometimes the leaves and small branches of the fig tree were used to communicate an agreeable flavour.59 The same writer has transmitted to us a very simple process, much in use in his time, for preserving cheese.
