Make as above, and line a basin with the paste to- lerably thin ; fill with the fruit and cover it; tie a cloth over tight, and boil till the fruit shall be done enough. Yeast or Suffolk Dumplings. Make a very light dough with yeast, as for bread, but with milk instead of water, and put salt. Let it rise an hour before the fire. Twenty minutes before you are to serve, have ready a large stew-pan of boiling water; make the dough in- to balls, the size of a middling apple ; throw them in, and boil twenty minutes. If you doubt whether done enough, stick a clean fork into one, and if it come out clear, it is done. The way to eat them is to tear them apart on the top with two forks, for they become heavy by their own steam. Eat immediately with meat, or sugar and butter, or salt. A Charlotte. Cut as many very thin slices of white bread as will cover the bottom and line the sides of a baking-dish, but first rub it thick with butter. Put apples, in thin slices into the dish, in layers, till full, strewing sugar between and bits of butter. In the meantime soak as many thin slices of bread as will cover the whole, in warm milk, over which lay a plate, and a weight to keep the bread close on the apples. Bake slowly three hours. To a middling-sized dish use half a pound of batter in tlie whole.
