It be- hoves every person to be extremely careful whom he takes into his service 3 to be very minute in investi- gating the character he receives, and equally cautious and scrupulously just in giving one to others. Were this attended to, many bad people would be incapaci- tated for doing mischief, by abusing the trust reposed in them. It may be fairly asserted that the robbery or waste, which is but a milder epithet for the unfaith- fulness of a servant, will be laid to the charge of that master or mistress who, knowing, or having well- founded suspicions of, such faults, is prevailed upon by false pity, or entreaty, to slide him into another place. There are, however, some who are unfortu- nately capricious, and often refuse to give a character because they are displeased that a servant leaves their service: but this is unpardonable, and an absolute robbery, servants having no inheritance, and depending on their fair name for employment. To refuse coun- tenance to the evil, and to encourage the good, servant, are actions due to society at large 3 and such as are honest, frugal, and attentive to their duties should be liberally rewarded, which would encourage merit, and inspire servants with zeal to acquit themselves. It may be proper to observe that a retributive justice usually marks persons in that station sooner or later even in this world.
