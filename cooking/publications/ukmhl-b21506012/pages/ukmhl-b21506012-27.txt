The patois is easily acquired, and you will soon find yourself interpreting the cherished mysteries of Erancatelli or Gouffb in the pigeon English of Madras with marvellous fluency. You will even talk of “ putting that troople,” “ mashing bones all,” “ minching,” “ chimmering,” &c., &c., without a blush. There can be no doubt that in our Ramasamy we possess admirable materials out of which to form a good cook. The work comes to him, as it were, of its own accord. But we should take heed lest he grow up at random, clinging affectionately to the ancient barbarisms of his forefathers. We should watch for his besetting sins, and root them out whenever they manifest themselves. Two of the chief of these are :— (a) To guess at quantities and weights without measur- ing the one, or weighing the other. (b) To proceed with the cooking of a meal without any reference to the clock, to finish things far too soon, and then to keejD them warm till wanted. We should remember that the fact of a dish having been once successfully presented by nb means insures that it will appear so again unless the artist be reminded de novo of the secrets of its composition.
