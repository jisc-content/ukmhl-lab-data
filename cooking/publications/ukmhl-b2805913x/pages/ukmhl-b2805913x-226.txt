Chapteb XI. PUDDINGS. 777. Neun-Loth Pudding ”—A PJiine Pudding. This celebrated Rhenish “ neun-Ioth ” pudding is a general favourite, and is made as follows :—Stir half a pint of milk into four ounces and a half of fine flour and four ounces and a half of powdered loaf sugar. Put into a stew-pan four ounces and a half of butter and half a pint of milk. TVhen this is hot and the butter melted, stir in the other milk and flour; keep the mixture stirred over the fire till it boils and thickens, then turn it out in a pan to cool. Stir in the yolks of nine eggs, four ounces and a half of almonds blanched and pounded, and, lastly, the nine egg-whites whisked to a stiff snow. Stir all briskly together, butter a mould or basin, fill it, and boil the pudding an hour and a half. The water must not cease boiling. Serve arrack or white wine sauce with it. 778. “ Kastanien Pudding ”— Chestnut Pudding. Cut a little cross on the point of each chestnut rind. Put them on the fire in boiling water, and let them boil till soft enough to peel. Take ofl' the rinds and inner brown skins. Pound in a mortar a pound and a half of them. In the mean- time, simmer a quarter of a stick of vanilla in half a pint of milk and let it cool. Stir a quarter of a pound of butter to cream. Add to it a quarter of a pound of powdered sugar and the yolks of six eggs. Stir these well with the cooled milk, then mix in the pounded chestnuts, and, lastly, the whites of the six eggs whipped to a snow. Boil in a buttered mould an hour and a half, and serve it with a white pudding sauce.
