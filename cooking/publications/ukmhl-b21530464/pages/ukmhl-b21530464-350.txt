Pour water on it, and let it lie to acquire some hardness before you i work it; then change the water, and beat it with flat t boards so perfectly that not the least taste of the butter- milk remain, and that the water, which must be often i changed, shall be quite clear in colour. It requires more working ; in hot than in cold weather; but in neither should be left with a particle of buttermilk, or a sour taste, as is • sometimes done. To preserve Butler. Take two parts of the best common salt, one part: good loaf-sugar, and one part saltpetre; beat them i well together. To sixteen ounces of butter thoroughly cleansed from the milk, put one ounce of this composi- tion ; work it well, and pot down, when become firm and i cold. The butter thus preserved is the better for keeping, and should not be used under a month. This article should be kept from the air, and is best in pots of the best glazed earth, that will hold from ten to fourteen pounds each. To preserve Butler for Winter, the best ivay. When the butter has been prepared as above directed, . take two parts of the best common salt, one part of good loaf-sugar, and one part of saltpetre, beaten and blended well together. Of this composition put one ounce to sixteen ounces of butter, and work it well together in a mass. Press it into the pans after the butter is become cool; for friction, though it be not touched by the hands, will soften it. The pans should hold ten or twelve pounds each.
