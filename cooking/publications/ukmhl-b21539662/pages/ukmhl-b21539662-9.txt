7 THE CULINARY POSSIBILITIES OF PLASMON. {From "Food and Cookery," August, 1903, page 208, edited by Mr. C. Hermann Senn ) Since the science of dietetics has become more intimately allied with the art of cookery, it has been recog^nised that the so-called “ rich foods ” are not always the most nourishing. That is to say, there are many substances which, while most useful as stimulants and producers of fat, are practically devoid of all flesh-forming and waste- repairing properties. Such foods taken in excess are positively harmful, load- ing the human system with substances difficult to eliminate and which induce disease. At the other end of the scale are starchy and watery foods, of less nutrient value. Of course, the aim in planning a dietary, or cooking a meal, must be to properly choose different foods so as to provide a well-balanced ration.
