Pot into a sauce-pan one ounce of patent cocoa, and one quart of water ; cover it, and when it boils, set it by the side of the fire to simmer for two hours. It is sometimes made in a larger quantity, poured from the sediment, and boiled up as it is required. CHOCOLATE. Scrape down one square of chocolate, and mix it with one pint of boiling milk, or milk and water; then mill or stir it well, and serve it with the froth. OATPIEAL GRUEL. Mix in a bason two table-spoonfuls of oatmeal with a little cold water; then pour on it about a quart of boiling water, stir it well, and let it settle for a few minutes; pour olF the water into a sauce-pan, and boil it for ten or fifteen minutes, stirring it, and taking off the scum as it rises. BARLET WATER. Wash two ounces of pearl-barley in water; boil it for a few minutes in a little fresh water; pour that off, and add a quart more; when it boils, let it simmer for nearly an hour, and then strain it. It may be sweetened with sugar, and seasoned with a little of the peel and juice of a lemon. APPLE WATER. Cut three or four large apples into slices; put them into a jug, and pour a quart of boiling water over them ; cover the jug. When quite cold, strain and sweeten it, and add a littleiemon juice. COOLING DRINK. A PALATABLE and cooling drink may be made by pouring hot water over slices of lemon; when cold, to be strained and sweetened. BEEF TEA. Cut a piece of lean beaf into small thin bits ; boil it with a quart of water in a closely-covered sauce-pan ; let it sim- mer for an hour, then strain, and serve it with salt.
