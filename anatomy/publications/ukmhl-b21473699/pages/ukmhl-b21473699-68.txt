To be placed in offi- cial situations of this kind, to have these, or such questions proposed, and yet to be totally unprepared for an answer, must be highly distressing to a per- son of any sensibility or spirit, and who, than be re- duced to such a dilemma, would not rather undergo a thousand inconveniencies in obtaining that neces- sary degree of information which both his honour and the duty of his profession point out to him. But should an operation be found necessary, a new train of embarrassments must ensue: Information alone will not be sufficient; that dexterity of hand, which can only be acquired by practice in dissection, and that firmness of mind, and quickness at expedi- ents, resulting from the habit of being familiar with such scenes, are qualifications equally indispens- able.
