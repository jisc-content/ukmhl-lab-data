INTRODUCTION. The particular branch of surgical practice that forms the subject of the following practical observ- ations, is certainly one of the highest importance, whether it is considered with regard to the com- fort or the safety of the patient; neither is the humane attention, the professional skill, or the ma- nual dexterity of the Surgeon, more frequently called forth, than on those occasions in which the urinary organs are affected with disease. The concealed situation of most of the parts concerned, the frequent ambiguity of symptoms, and the great length of time that these complaints in some instances continue, are so many fertile sources of obscurity, and they have powerfully tended to retard the progress of our knowledge in this particular department of pathology.
