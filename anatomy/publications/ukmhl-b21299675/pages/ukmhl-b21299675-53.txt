The pericardium in this case, contained more than a quart of common pus. When that part of the pericardium is inflamed which forms the immediate co- vering of the heart, the muscular substance of the latter is occasionally inflamed to some depth. When the pericardium, or any other membrane lining a circumscribed cavity, is inflamed, and a layer of coagulable lymph is formed upon its surface, there is a peculiar disposition given to the coagulable lymph by the action of the small vessels which pour it out. This peculiar disposi- tion is its tendency to immediate coagula- tion. As soon as the coagulable lymph is thrown out upon an inflamed surface, one must suppose that it immediately coagu- lates.
