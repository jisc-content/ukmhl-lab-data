The for- mation of the pus depends on a certain state of action in the vessels of the pleura, or more probably of the layer of the coagu- Jable lymph which covers it. The pus may either be accumulated in the whole cavity of the chest, or may be confined to a part of it by adhesions taking place between the lungs and the pleura, which invests the ribs and the intercostal spaces. When pus is evacuated into the cavity of the chest by the bursting of an abscess in the lungs, it is almost always confined within certain limits by adhesions. In cases of empyema, for the most part, there is not any particular ap- pearance of the chest observable on the outside: there is sometimes however a full- ness to be perceived externally on the side where the matter is accumulated, and even occasionally an evident swelling between two of the ribs, as of matter pointing.
