They are at first very small, being not larger than the heads of very small pins, and in this case are fre- quently accumulated in small clusters. The smaller tubercles of a cluster probably grow together, and form one larger tubercle. The most ordinary size of tubercles is about that of a garden pea, but they are subject in this respect to much variety. They adhere pretty closely to the substance of the lungs, have no peculiar covering or capsule, and have little or no vascularity. When cut into, they are found to consist of a white smooth substance, having great firmness, and often contain in part a thick curdly pus. When a tubercle is almost entirely changed into pus, it appears like a white capsule, in which the pus is lodged. When several tu- bercles of considerable.size are grown toge- ther, so as to form a pretty large tuberculated mass, pus is very generally found upon cut- ting into it.
