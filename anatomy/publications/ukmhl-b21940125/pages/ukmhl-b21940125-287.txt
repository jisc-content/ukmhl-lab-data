In this case its coats are often a good deal thickened, and this arises partly from the pressure against the gall-bladder, in consequence of the accumulation of the stones, and partly from the efforts of the contractile power of the gall-bladder to ex- pel them. The number of stones accumu- lated in the gall-bladder is sometimes very great; above a thousand have been taken out of one gall-bladder, which are pre- served in Dr. Hunter’s collection. When there is a solitary gall-stone in the gall- bladder, it is occasionally very large; I have known an instance of one which was fully the size of a hen’s egg. When there is but one gall-stone either in the gall-bladder, or in the biliary ducts, it is generally of an oval shape ; when there is a considerable number, they acquire, by rubbing upon each other, a great many sides and angles.
