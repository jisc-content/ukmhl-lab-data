Still, however, a person well ac- quainted with the natural appearance ol the dura mater, would be as much struck with the difference of its appearance when it is inflamed, as he would be with that of any other part of the body. The dura mater during a stateof inflam- mation sometimes forms a layer of coagu- lable lymph, which adheres upon its inner surface like an adventitious membrane; but this is very uncommon. When the dura mater is inflamed, adhe- sions are sometimes formed between it and the other membranes of the brain, so that for a considerable extent of surface they adhere together ; but this appearance of disease is also very rare. The reason why diseased adhesions between the membranes of the brain are rare, depends, probably, upon coagulablelymph being seldom thrown out upon their surface when they are in- flamed.
