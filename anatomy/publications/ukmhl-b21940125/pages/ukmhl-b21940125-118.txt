In opening into the chest, it'is not un- usual to find that the lungs do not collapse, but that they fill up the cavity completely on each side of the heart. When examined, their cells appear full of air, so that there is seen upon the surface of the lungs im- mediately under the pleura a prodigious number of small white vesicles. The branches of the trachea are often at the same time a good deal filled with a mucous fluid. Air Cells of the Lungs enlarged. The lungs are sometimes, although I be- lieve very rarely, formed into pretty large cells, so as to resemble somewhat the lungs of an amphibious animal. Of this I have now seen three instances.
