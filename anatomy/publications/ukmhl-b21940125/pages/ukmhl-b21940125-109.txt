That portion of the lungs which is in- flamed becomes considerably heavier than in the natural state, from the accumulation of blood in its vessels, and the extravasation of the coagulable lymph ; it therefore fre- quently sinks in water. It feels like a solid substance when pressed by the fingers, and there is no crackling of air as in the healthy structure. This however will be more or less marked, according to the degree of the inflammation. The pleura covering the inflamed portion of the lungs is also commonly affected with inflammation ; it is crowded with fine red vessels, and has generally lying upon it a layer of coagulable lymph. This inflamed state of the lungs is to be distinguished from blood accumulated in some part of them after death in conse- quence of gravitation.
