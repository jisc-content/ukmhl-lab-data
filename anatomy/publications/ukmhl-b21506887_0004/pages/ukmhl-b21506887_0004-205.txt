PART 11. OF BIRDS. OF THE BONES. The SJcelefon of Birds has a considerable uniformity in the whole class, and bears a strong analogy to that of Mammalia; but the large Bones, in Birds of whatever shape, are destitute of Marrow in their inter- nal Cavities, in place of which they contain air, and communicate with the Lungs by .considerable apertures^ Foetal Birds, however, or such as have just quitted the Shell, have their Bones filled with Bloody Marrow, which, in general, is soon absorbed, and, in Aquatic Birds, the greater part disappears. The SJcuUy in this class of Animals, has no Sutures in the Adult state, but is in a manner consolidated into one piece. In young Birds, there is a kind of False Sutures, but these are soon obliterated. The Cranium is of a Cellular texture, and particularly so in some, as an Eagle and Owly by which the strength and bulk of the part is increased, and the Body rendered specifically lighter.
