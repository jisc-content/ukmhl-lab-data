KALOGYNOMIA, OR THE 138 But, though I lov’d you well, I woo’d you not;— And yet, good faith, I wish’d myself a man ; Or that we women had men’s privilege Of speaking first.—Sweet, bid me hold my tongue ; For, in this rapture I shall surely speak The thing I shall repent.—See, see, your silence. Cunning in dumbness, from my weakness draws My very soul of counsel.—Stop my mouth. Act III, Scene 2. On this passage how true are Godwin’s reflections!—“What charming ingenuous- ness, what exquisite naivetd, what ravish- ing confusion of soul, are expressed in these words! We seem to perceive in them every fleeting thought as it rises in the mind of Cressida, at the same time that they delineate with equal skill all the beautiful timidity and innocent artifice which grace and consummate the feminine character.
