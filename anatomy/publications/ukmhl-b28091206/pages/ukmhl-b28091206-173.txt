LAWS OF FEMALE BEAUTY. II9 This prerogative is perhaps less to be attributed to his particular nature than to the advantages resulting from his industry. Sheltered from the rigours of the season and variations of the atmosphere, by the abode which he has been able to construct, and having it in his power to satisfy his physical wants by means of provision, which his foresight had accumulated, he can at all times, and under equal advan- tages, partake of the enjoyments of love. This is especially the case, among the more opulent classes, where good living, leisure and the habitual intercourse of the two sexes, excite the imagination and be- stow a new and more extensive influence upon these wants. In the same manner, domestic animals, which we have in some measure abstracted from external influence, are productive almost indifferently in all seasons.
